Moulding and Casting

The process of moulding and casting is very simple. A mould could be thought of as a cavity or shell which is made from the imprints of any given object. The mould could be made out of materials such as moudling sand, rubber, clay, resin and many more. Once the mould is made, we then continue on with casting which is basically to add material, usually in liquid state, into the mould and let it take the mould's shape and harden before extracting it out. Casting material can range from metals such as brass to lighter materials such as plastic and wax.

In this modules, we were to make a pre-mould out of foam, then create the mould using silicon, and finally finish it with adding our material of choice for casting. In my case, I chose wax as I wanted to make a candle.

Making the pre-mould

To make the pre-mould, I need to design a 3D model of the object I want to cast, along with a shell so that I can make my mould. This pre-mould would be cut using the CNC machine in the FabLab (stepcraft420V2 currently). If you have not seen how I operated the machine you could refer to the PCB Design and Fabrication page of my website where I showed how I used the CNC machine to create my own PCB. Using it to create my pre-mould is not too different other than how I generated the Gcode.

The first step for me was to create a 3D model of my pre-mould using Fusion 360. I measured the piece of foam provided before hand to ensure that the size of my pre-mould could fit. The height is especially important in this case. As you can see from above, the heart-shaped candle that I eventually want to cast is in the centre of the pre-mould, and I have also added a shell around to create a boundary for the mould.

Next on the list, I have to tweak the settings for the CNC machine, basically telling it how I want it to be milled, how detailed it should be and the fast it should go. I used the settings recommended by the lecturer and changed some to better suit my design.

With all that done, I just create the nc programme using the STEPCRAFT UCCNC post processing library and everything is good to go.

I started the CNC milling machine up and started my programme. At this point, setting the z-axis zero accurately is not too important and can be done manually by slowly lowering the drill bit. The important part is to ensure that the drill bite x and y axis are set to the centre of the foam so that it does not cut beyond the boundaries of the foam block. Casting the Silicon Mould

Once the milling ends, I took out my pre-moulds and got ready to use silicon to cast my actual mould. Silicon is great because its flexible and easy obtain. I was provided with two cans, the first can contains the silicon itself and the second can contains a hardener. I refered to a datasheet to know the correct ratio to mix the two. I used a weighing scale to measure weight to ensure my ratio is as close to the suggested ratio as possible.

I then mixed them for about 5 minutes before pouring them into the foam pre-mould. I ensured not to stir for too long as it may affect the curing (hardening) process later. The curing process takes about 1 full day according to the datasheet, but in my experience, depending on the size of the mould it took up to 2 full days before my moulds were fully cured.

I extracted the silicon moulds out of the foam and scraped away as much foam inside as possible to prevent the foam from disrupting the casting process.

Casting the candle

I wanted to make candles, therefore I had to find a wax along with finding a way to heat it up. The decided to use an old unused candle I found, aluminium foil and a lighter to heat up the wax and some cloth rope as a wick. As you can see from above I wore a rubber glove to take precaution against the aluminium foil getting too hot for me to hold.

With the wax melted, I poured it into the silicon mould and let it rest to allow the wax to solidify.
